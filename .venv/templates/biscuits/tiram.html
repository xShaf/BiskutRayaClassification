Biscuit: Kuih Tiram

Recipe

  • 500g wheat flour
  • 2 tablespoons margarine (melted)
  • 1 teaspoon salt
  • Cold water (as needed)

Glue Mixture (for brushing)

  • Cornstarch + cooking oil (mix until texture resembles condensed milk)
  • Powdered sugar (for coating)

Instructions

  1. Prepare the dough: Melt margarine. Pour into wheat flour. Mix well until it resembles breadcrumbs.
  2. Form the dough: Dissolve salt in water and add cold water little by little until it forms a non-sticky dough. Let rest for 30 minutes.
  3. Make the glue mixture: Combine cornstarch and cooking oil. Mix well until it reaches a condensed milk-like consistency. (No exact measurements needed - aim for not too thick nor too thin).
  4. Shape the cookies: Roll dough thinly, cut neatly, brush with glue mixture, then roll up. Cut the rolled dough into small pieces and flatten slightly.
  5. Frying: Fry in moderately hot oil until crispy. Drain using a strainer, then place on paper towels to remove excess oil.
  6. Finishing: When completely cooled, coat the kuih tiram with powdered sugar.

History of Kuih Tiram

Kuih Tiram, named after its distinctive oyster-shell shape (tiram means "oyster" in Malay), is a traditional crispy wafer cookie that originated from the Malay communities of Peninsular Malaysia. Unlike its name suggests, it contains no seafood but gets its signature texture from a unique cornstarch glue coating.

Origins & Cultural Significance

1. Coastal Inspiration (19th Century)
First created in Malacca and Selangor coastal towns, where oyster shells were abundant. The folded shape mimicked oyster shells found at local markets, making it a creative example of culinary symbolism.

2. Royal Kitchen Heritage
Some food historians trace kuih tiram to Malay royal kitchens, where intricate folding techniques were perfected. The cornstarch glue method (gam) was developed to create its signature crispness without eggs, making it different from Western wafers.

3. Festival Staple
Became a must-have for Hari Raya because its long shelf life suited festive preparations. The powdered sugar coating symbolizes purity and celebration, while the golden-brown color represents prosperity.
