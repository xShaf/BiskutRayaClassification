Biscuit: Batang Buruk

Recipe

  • A pack of spring roll wrappers (kulit popia)
  • 500 grams of mung beans (kacang hijau)
  • 10 tablespoons of sugar
  • 1 egg (use only the egg white)
  • 2 large cups of milk powder
  • Large straw (used for shaping or rolling)

Instructions

  1. First, dry-fry the mung beans (without oil) until fragrant. Let them cool, then blend the mung beans and sugar together until fine.
  2. Cut and roll the spring roll wrappers using a straw. Apply a little egg white at the edge of the wrapper to seal it so it doesn’t come undone.
  3. Sun-dry the rolled wrappers until dry, then deep-fry them. Drain the oil and let them cool.
  4. Next, mix the previously blended ingredients (mung beans and sugar) with the milk powder.
  5. Finally, fill each fried spring roll wrapper with the filling and arrange them neatly in a container.

History

Batang buruk is famous for its unattractiveness. From its outer appearance to its name, kuih batang buruk (literally “rotten stick”) is one of the ugliest traditional biskut raya ever created in the Malay world. The name batang buruk possibly comes from its appearance, which resembles a tree branch but is coated with mung bean powder, making it look like a decayed stick. When compared to other more visually appealing kuih, kuih batang buruk still survives to this day due to its unique, sweet, and delicious taste.
