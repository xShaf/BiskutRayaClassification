Biscuit: Kuih Bangkit

Recipe

  • 3 cups cassava flour (sifted)
  • ½ cup wheat flour
  • 1 cup thick coconut milk
  • 1 cup powdered sugar (icing sugar)
  • 2 grade B egg yolks
  • ½ teaspoon baking powder

Instructions

  1. Prepare the dry ingredients: Lightly toast the cassava flour and wheat flour, then sift them. Transfer to a mixing bowl, add the coconut milk, and mix well.
  2. Form the dough: Add the egg yolks and mix until combined. Knead the mixture into a smooth dough.
  3. Shape the cookies: Roll out the dough to a medium thickness (not too thin or too thick). Cut out shapes using your preferred cookie cutter.
  4. Bake: Arrange the cookies on a baking tray. Bake at 150°C (300°F) for 25–30 minutes or until fully cooked. Let them cool completely before storing in an airtight container.

History of Kuih Bangkit

Kuih bangkit is a delicate, melt-in-your-mouth cookie that holds a special place in Malay, Peranakan (Nyonya), and Indonesian culinary traditions. Its name comes from the Malay word "bangkit," meaning "to rise"—referring to how the cookies puff up slightly when baked.

Origins & Cultural Significance

1. Malay & Indonesian Roots
Believed to have originated from the Malay Archipelago, particularly in Malaysia and Indonesia (where it's called kue bangkit). Traditionally made for festive occasions like Hari Raya (Eid), Chinese New Year (among Peranakan communities), and weddings.

2. Peranakan (Nyonya) Influence
The Straits Chinese (Baba Nyonya) community refined the recipe, incorporating coconut milk and tapioca flour for a lighter texture. Often shaped using intricate wooden molds (like floral or animal designs) for symbolic meaning (prosperity, luck).

3. Dutch Colonial Connection
Some historians suggest Dutch colonists in Indonesia may have influenced the cookie’s buttery texture, similar to European shortbread.
